Comfort Women

What are comfort women?

Comfort women were women forced into sexual slavery by the Japanese military during World War II. They were subjected to horrific abuse and trauma. Many survivors have spoken out about their experiences.

Kim Hak-Sun

Kim Hak-sun was a South Korean woman who gained significant recognition for her courage in speaking out about her experiences as a "comfort woman" during World War II. Born in 1924, she was one of the many women forced into sexual slavery by the Japanese military. In 1991, she became the first to publicly testify about her suffering, which played a crucial role in raising global awareness about the issue and advocating for justice and compensation. Her bravery helped galvanize the movement for acknowledgment and reparations for former comfort women, highlighting the need for historical redress and support for survivors.

Shirota Suzuko

"I was their slave." In 1938, at the age of 17, Shirota Suzuko's father sold her to the Japanese military to pay off his debt. Until the war ended, Shirota had to serve Japanese soldiers as a sex slave at comfort stations in Taiwan and Saipan. From 1955, Suzuko lived at a shelter for victims od sexual violence for 20 years and published a book about her past experience as a 'Comfort Woman' in 1971.
